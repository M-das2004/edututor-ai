CHAPTER 1: LABORATORY SAFETY RULES
=====================================

General Safety Rules
---------------------
ALWAYS follow these rules in the science laboratory:

1. Never eat or drink in the laboratory
2. Always wear a lab coat/apron when handling chemicals
3. Wash hands thoroughly before and after experiments
4. Never smell chemicals directly — use the "wafting" technique (wave hand over bottle)
5. If chemicals spill on skin, immediately wash with lots of water
6. Keep hair tied back and loose clothes tucked in
7. Never point the mouth of a heated test tube at yourself or others
8. Broken glassware must be reported immediately — do not touch broken glass
9. Know the location of the fire extinguisher and first aid box
10. Follow teacher's instructions exactly

Common Lab Symbols and Meanings
---------------------------------
⚠ Caution/Warning — be careful
☠ Toxic — do not inhale or touch
🔥 Flammable — keep away from fire
⚡ High voltage — do not touch
☣ Biohazard — biological risk

CHAPTER 2: IMPORTANT EXPERIMENTS (CLASS 9-10)
================================================

Experiment 1: Verifying Ohm's Law (Physics)
--------------------------------------------
Aim: To verify Ohm's Law and find the resistance of a wire

Materials needed:
- Battery (6V), rheostat, ammeter, voltmeter, connecting wires, resistance wire

Procedure:
1. Connect circuit: Battery → Rheostat → Ammeter → Resistance wire → back to battery
2. Connect voltmeter across (parallel to) the resistance wire
3. Vary the rheostat to change current; record Voltmeter (V) and Ammeter (I) readings
4. Record at least 5 different readings

Observations (sample):
| V (Volts) | I (Amperes) | V/I (Ohms) |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| 1.0       | 0.1         | 10        |
| 2.0       | 0.2         | 10        |
| 3.0       | 0.3         | 10        |

Conclusion: V/I = constant (= Resistance), which verifies Ohm's Law.

Experiment 2: Testing for Starch in Food (Biology)
----------------------------------------------------
Aim: To test for the presence of starch using iodine solution

Materials: Food samples (bread, rice, potato, apple), iodine solution, test tubes

Procedure:
1. Take small pieces of each food sample
2. Add 2-3 drops of iodine solution to each
3. Observe color change

Results:
- BLUE-BLACK color: Starch is PRESENT
- No color change: Starch is ABSENT

Expected results:
- Bread: Blue-black ✓ (starch present)
- Rice: Blue-black ✓ (starch present)  
- Potato: Blue-black ✓ (starch present)
- Apple: No change ✗ (no starch)

Conclusion: Iodine solution is used as an indicator for starch. Blue-black color confirms presence of starch.

Experiment 3: Acid-Base Indicators (Chemistry)
------------------------------------------------
Aim: To study the behavior of acids and bases using indicators

Materials: HCl, NaOH, litmus solution, phenolphthalein, test tubes

Observations:
| Substance    | Litmus Color | Phenolphthalein | Nature  |
|-------------|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| HCl solution | Red          | Colorless       | Acid    |
| NaOH solution| Blue         | Pink            | Base    |
| Distilled Water| No change  | Colorless       | Neutral |
| Lemon juice  | Red          | Colorless       | Acid    |
| Baking soda  | Blue         | Pink            | Base    |

Conclusion: Litmus turns red in acids and blue in bases. Phenolphthalein turns pink in bases and remains colorless in acids.

CHAPTER 3: WRITING LAB REPORTS
=================================

Structure of a Lab Report
---------------------------
Every lab experiment report must include:

1. AIM: What are you trying to find out? (1-2 sentences)
2. MATERIALS: List all equipment and chemicals used
3. PROCEDURE: Step-by-step instructions (use numbered points)
4. PRECAUTIONS: What safety/accuracy precautions did you take?
5. OBSERVATIONS: What did you see? (use tables/diagrams)
6. CALCULATIONS: Show all working (if applicable)
7. RESULT: What did you find? (1-2 sentences)
8. CONCLUSION: What does this prove? Connect to theory.
9. SOURCES OF ERROR: What could have made the results inaccurate?

Common Mistakes in Lab Reports
--------------------------------
1. Writing "I did..." instead of passive voice ("The wire was connected...")
2. Forgetting units (write 10 Ω not just 10)
3. Not drawing neat, labeled diagrams
4. Skipping precautions section
5. Writing vague conclusions ("We found that the experiment worked")

Always write: "The experiment proves/verifies that [theory]..."
